Aramaki Lab Research

2024 - Present | Northeastern University

Context

During my junior year, I took Multi-Messenger Astrophysics taught by Professor Tsuogo Aramaki. Beyond being deeply interested in the class material, I found his research work at the Aramaki Lab cutting-edge and asked for the opportunity to learn more and potentially get involved.

I was thrilled to learn that they needed a mechanical engineer, and I could promptly join the team. For the remainder of that semester, I worked 7+ hours a week under the guidance of PhD student Jiancheng Zeng.

Background

Our team at the Aramaki Lab is developing two CCD-based detectors in parallel:

  • GRAMS - Gamma-Ray and AntiMatter Survey
  • MicroGRAMS - Smaller-scale prototype detector

The goal is to track the trajectory and energy deposition of cosmic particles to identify their characteristics and origins. These experiments target the MeV energy range, a region with a significant research gap, and aim to enable indirect observations of antimatter.

Technical Details

The detector is a Liquid Argon Time Projection Chamber (LArTPC) that employs SiPM (Silicon Photomultiplier) technology. When a particle enters the chamber, the SiPMs detect scintillation light produced in the plastic scintillator, while also capturing the ionized electrons.

My Role & Contributions

The Challenge

Before our flight in May 2025, our team needs to conduct routine tests to ensure reliable data acquisition, control of environmental factors, and proper heat transfer of components. To perform these tests, we need to immerse the TPC in a liquid argon chamber at the lab—but no mounting solution existed to do so safely.

My Solution

As the team's mechanical engineer, I designed and 3D printed a component that could:

  1. Interface securely with the flange of the liquid argon chamber
  2. Avoid interference with surrounding obstacles (tubes, pipes, and other sensitive components)
  3. Hold the extremely delicate detector with high stability, preventing even the smallest positional shifts that could compromise data quality

CAD Model of the TPC holder (LEFT) and the implemented, white, 3D-printed holder (RIGHT)

Process & Iteration

The design process required many iterations, as there was little existing CAD to reference and significant trial and error was needed to achieve the correct fits and tolerances. I took ownership of weekly prototyping, which also gave me hands-on experience with rigorous 3D printer maintenance and troubleshooting.

The implemented TPC holder in use at the Aramaki Lab

Results

By the end of the semester, I had fully implemented and tested my design, and it is still being used in the lab today! The mounting solution has enabled consistent and reliable testing procedures, contributing to the project's progress toward the May 2025 flight.

Key Achievements

  • Successfully designed and implemented critical lab infrastructure
  • Enabled routine testing procedures that were previously impossible
  • Gained experience in aerospace-grade component design
  • Strengthened interdisciplinary collaboration skills
